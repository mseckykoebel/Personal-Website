what is adaptive learning good for?

In the previous section, I went over numerous shortcomings of adaptive learning as it currently exists, and how it is being applied. You may be asking yourself: is adaptive learning good for anything?

I mentioned the need for context, as well as the presence of having the intention and intrinsic motivation to learn a subject. There is a difference between cultivating interest and exercising interest. When motivation and intent are present, and someone knows what they are doing, I think this is where adaptive learning shines.

The Importance of Insight and Analytics

Teachers value the ability to understand their students. For me, what adaptive learning does best is its ability to process and analyze the data they collect on individual students, and visually display it to teachers.

This for me is the biggest selling point, assuming I am a teacher of a classroom that understands how to use adaptive learning, and correctly distributes material to my students. I could confidently shift my focus to what is needed more generally after getting insight into what my class is succeeding in, and what they are having a hard time with.

Source: CENTURY Tech's learning analytics dashboard.

Now, I have pointed fingers at how the data is collected, but assuming the data was accurate and delivered in a way that is easy to interpret, I think this is very relevant in the scope of a classroom.

Moving Through Mistakes, and Not Around Them

The theory of "growth" and "fixed" mindsets is based on the notion that individuals with "growth" mindsets possess the capacity to persevere through their mistakes and to recognize the fact that one can learn and make progress in learning when a mistake is made. Because those with "growth" mindsets see intelligence as a function of the amount of work one puts in, and not natural ability, mistakes are not seen as personal reflections of intelligence, but as basic obstacles in the process of learning something.

Adaptive learning, in its function, will show you material that it confidently thinks you already know, and this can help jog your memory on material that is related to solving the problem, leading to perseverance. This is helpful, as it can show you the area of review that you should be concerned with to progress. Now, yes, this is still on the basic premise of solving problems, and it still rewards correct answers and the process of solving these problems, but at least the student has a "personal" knowledge base to work with that they can access if they need it.

After Motivation, Comes Growth - Adaptive Learning for the Seasoned Learner

I think adaptive learning has more of a home when learning context, motivation, and intention have already been established. Right away, I think of adults who want to learn a new skill and can log into a website, or use a service, that has a set of expectations that one has to meet. It is a big part of the backbone of online degrees and certification, and it is one of the best consequences of the existence of the internet - if you want to learn something, you can.

This comes with the premise that to seek out knowledge, you must have motivation and intention to seek out the knowledge. If you know yourself, and you know what you want to look for, on the internet, you can do it. I think adaptive learning in the scope of specific lessons can be effective because, in this setting, context and motivation do not need to be cultivated, as it is already present. Adaptive learning's reason for existing becomes clearer the less reliant on context and cultivating interest becomes.

This means that for adolescents, I think adaptive learning is at its weakest. However, when someone wants to learn something, and they know why they want to learn it, adaptive learning has its perks.

Conclusion - To the Future

I am a believer in technology - I believe that one day, technology, by some medium, will be able to understand the context in which learning occurs strong enough, and can be implemented affordably enough, so that education can finally be disrupted by technology on the macro scale.

But for now, learning science and artificial intelligence still need to work on their relationship, just like mindset research advocates and equality advocates. As far as teaching adolescents are concerned, I believe new innovative solutions are required that really hone the learning science research, and intentionally employ engineering practices that may not exactly overlap and latch on blindly to the "newest"technology. I want the future of adolescent education to not have as many holes and inconsistencies as adaptive learning does. And, of course, understanding has to be appreciated more than knowing.

As with any great solution, technology should not come before the problem. Identifying the weakest points, and designing solutions that directly try and address the issues of motivation, equity, and true personalization, while not being driven by the need to commodify our lives, needs to be the focus. Until a time when computers can mimic human intuition and can understand context, education needs to emphasize collaboration between the young brains of motivated and inquisitive peers, and not isolation, behind a highly-trained, impersonal guesser.
